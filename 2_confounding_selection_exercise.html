D-separation.

Reading independence and dependence off a causal graph. All exercises use the sprinkler network on the right — it stays visible as you work through each slide.

Chain   A → M → B

Influence flows through M. Conditioning on M blocks the path; not conditioning leaves it open.

Fork   A ← M → B

Common cause M. Conditioning on M blocks the path; not conditioning leaves it open.

Collider   A → M ← B

Blocked by default. Conditioning on M or any descendant of M opens the path.

Part 04

Fix the bias, in code.

You're a data analyst for an esports organization. The team's energy-drink sponsor claims their product improves in-match performance, and leadership wants an honest read before renewing the deal. You've pulled match records for 240 players — write the Python to check whether the effect is real.

What you're seeing

A first look at the data shows drinkers scoring noticeably higher than non-drinkers. But you notice something: Pro players reach for the drink far more often than amateur players do — it's practically part of their pre-match routine — and Pros already outscore amateurs regardless of what they drink. skill_tier is a confounder here: it affects both who drinks the sponsor's product and how well they score, so a raw comparison mixes the drink's real effect with the skill-tier effect.

🎯 Your task

Complete the two functions below. naive_effect computes the raw, biased comparison across the whole sample. stratified_effect computes the bias-corrected estimate by working within each skill_tier stratum before combining the results. Fill in all 5 highlighted blanks.

  • Step 1 — the naive difference: energy-drink average minus no-drink average, across everyone.
  • Step 2 — inside the loop, the condition selecting rows where a player did not drink an energy drink, within the current stratum.
  • Step 3 — the energy-drink effect within this one stratum: same shape as Step 1, computed on group instead of the whole df.
  • Step 4 — the weight for this stratum: how many players are in it.
  • Step 5 — combine every stratum's difference into one number, weighted by stratum size.

💡 This is exactly how you close a back-door path through a confounder: instead of comparing drinkers vs. non-drinkers across everyone, you compare them inside each level of the confounder, then combine those comparisons.

# df: player_id, skill_tier ("Pro"/"Amateur"), energy_drink (True/False), match_score

def naive_effect(df):
    drink_mean    = df[df.energy_drink].match_score.mean()
    no_drink_mean = df[~df.energy_drink].match_score.mean()
    return 


def stratified_effect(df):
    differences, weights = [], []

    for tier, group in df.groupby('skill_tier'):
        drink_mean    = group[group.energy_drink].match_score.mean()
        no_drink_mean = group[].match_score.mean()

        differences.append()
        weights.append()

    weighted_sum  = sum(d * w for d, w in zip(differences, weights))
    total_weight  = sum(weights)
    return
💡 Start with the intuition before the syntax:
  • Step 1: compute the raw energy-drink gap across the whole sample. This is the biased estimate to compare against.
  • Step 2: inside each skill tier, select the non-drinker rows the same way you selected drinker rows — use ~group.energy_drink.
  • Step 3: compute the per-tier effect just like the naive difference, but only within the current group.
  • Step 4: count how many players are in the current group using len(group). That number is the weight for this tier.
  • Step 5: return the weighted average: divide the total weighted difference by the sum of all weights.

This is how stratification removes the confounder: instead of comparing all drinkers and non-drinkers together, you compare them within each skill tier and then combine those tier-specific effects.

The end.
